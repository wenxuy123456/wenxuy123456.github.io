Experiment 07

Carbon Snake

Introduction

What starts as a small pile of solid reactants can grow into a long, black, twisting structure. The Carbon Snake experiment shows how decomposition, combustion, and gas production can work together to create a dramatic expanding shape.

Background Information

Carbon Snake is a popular demonstration because the reaction looks alive as it grows upward. The black material is mostly carbon-rich residue, while the expansion happens because gases are produced and push the soft material outward.

A common version uses sugar and baking soda. Heat causes sugar to break down and leave behind carbon, while baking soda decomposes to release carbon dioxide gas. Together, the carbon and gas create the snake-like column.

Purpose

The objective of this experiment is to observe decomposition, combustion, and gas expansion by creating a carbon-rich structure from simple reactants.

Materials

  • Heat-safe tray or ceramic dish
  • Sand
  • Sugar
  • Baking soda, NaHCO3
  • Alcohol fuel or another teacher-approved fuel source
  • Lighter or matches
  • Stirring tool or spoon
  • Safety goggles and gloves

Safety Note

  1. Wear safety goggles and gloves during the demonstration.
  2. Only perform this experiment with adult or teacher supervision.
  3. Work in a well-ventilated area because smoke and fumes may form.
  4. Do not touch the product until it has cooled completely.
  5. Keep flammable materials away from the reaction area.

Experimental Procedure

  1. Place sand in a heat-safe tray to form a stable base.
  2. Mix sugar and baking soda together in a small container.
  3. Make a small mound or depression in the sand.
  4. Add the sugar and baking soda mixture to the center.
  5. Add a small amount of approved fuel around the mixture.
  6. Ignite the fuel carefully and step back.
  7. Observe as the black carbon snake slowly grows.
  8. Let the product cool completely before cleaning up.

How Does This Work?

When heated, sugar begins to decompose and forms carbon-rich material. This gives the final product its dark black color.

Baking soda also decomposes when heated. It produces carbon dioxide gas, water vapor, and sodium carbonate. The gases become trapped in the soft carbon material and force it to expand upward.

The result is a lightweight, porous structure that looks like a growing snake. The reaction is not actually alive; it is the continuous production of gas pushing carbon outward.

Further Explorations

You can try changing the ratio of sugar to baking soda and compare how tall, wide, or fragile the carbon snake becomes. You can also observe how the amount of fuel affects reaction speed and final shape.
